My Learning Journey: From Survivor to Accessibility Champion

This is where I document my journey through web development, accessibility, and self-advocacy. Each entry will include both code concepts and what they mean in the context of surviving and thriving, with disability and trauma.

Introduction to HTML & Real Accessibility

What I Learned: I began learning about HTML elements, structure, headings, links, images, and basic accessibility principles like using alt text for screen readers. Accessibility wasn't fully integrated into this stage. ARIA roles and attributes weren't covered at all, and I had no clue what aria-current was. I now realize this is where the foundations of accessibility are important, and should be introduced. I only began learning ARIA on my own after running into major barriers during an intro to JavaScript and form development. That's when surviving stopped being enough, and I had to build access just to participate.

I didn't become an accessibility advocate because I wanted to. I became one because I had no choice. In order to learn, build, and thrive, I had to make web work for me.

View my Box Model & Accessibility project, where I explore basic layout styling while reflecting on accessibility for screen readers and keyboard users.

Why it matters: I'm legally blind, but that's not always obvious. I wear glasses without lenses, not for vision correction, but for confidence. They don't improve my vision, but they help me feel seen. I didn't become an accessibility advocate because I wanted to. Curiosities & Accessible Brainstorming:

  • Why does alt text often feel like it's being written for compliance, instead of communication?

Assistive Tech Log:

I'm still adjusting to vision loss. I've only been legally blind since 2020. For assistive technology, I regularly alternate between VoiceOver on Mac , JAWS on Windows , and NVDA when JAWS breaks. Each screen reader has its own logic, with unique strengths and limitations, their own quirks. The learning curve is steep, and that's before you even get to the code. Built-in accessibility tools help, but knowing how the markup behaves under each system is what's making the biggest difference. I often have to use all three tools to navigate efficiently.

Resources & References:

  • MDN Web Docs: What is aria-current?
  • WebAIM Contrast Checker
  • W3C Alt Text Decision Tree
  • Axess Lab: 5 Myths About Alt Text

Note: I often hear advice to 'keep alt text short', but as someone who depends on it, I want the details. If there's a painting on a website, I don't just want "a woman." I want to know what she's wearing, how she's positioned, what the colors are like. Good alt text is not just compliance, it's storytelling in a way that allows the user to visualize.

Introduction to CSS & Styling Accessibly

Learning CSS was incredibly challenging for me. I had to find alternative ways to "see" what I was creating. CSS is all about styling - colors, layout, font-size, positioning - things that are vsiual by nature. My learning style had to shift dramatically. I leaned heavily upon my screen readers and borrowed volunteer human eyes to understand what was happening on the screen.

When CSS isn't accessible, it's not just an oversight. It can block someone entirely from learning or using we've built.

What I Learned:

  • CSS means "Cascading Style Sheets", and it controls how the HTML looks.
  • You can change fonts, colors, layouts, padding, margins, and more.
  • Selectors target what gets styled.
  • The "cascade" part means that the order and specificity of rules matter.

How to use Reader Mode:

  • Be My Eyes: A volunteer based app that helped me understand layout, color, and spacing.
  • NVDA Screen Reader was used to hear what my code sounded like.
  • JAWS Screen Reader also tested how navigation and styles were interpreted.
  • Reader Mode (try this!):
    • FireFox: Press F9 or click the page icon in the address bar.
    • Safari: Click the page icon on the far left side of the address bar (if available), or use the "View > Show Reader" menu.
